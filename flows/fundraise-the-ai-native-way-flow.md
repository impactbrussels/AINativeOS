# Fundraise the AI-Native Way Flow

> This flow prepares the evidence a raise rests on. It is not financial, investment, or legal
> advice, and it does not tell you whether, when, or from whom to raise. Take the deck, the numbers,
> and the terms to a qualified adviser. What this flow does is one thing: assemble proof that
> survives scrutiny, so that when you do raise, you raise on evidence rather than on a story.

A generalist investor funds the story. An AI-native investor funds the proof, because they have seen
the wrappers that demoed beautifully and died when the giant shipped the same feature for free. They
will run the Remove-the-AI test on you in the room. They will ask what compounds. They will probe
the eval. A deck built on hype meets that questioning and folds. This flow builds the deck the other
way round: every claim assembled as evidence first, the narrative laid over proof that already
stands. Four skills, in order, each gated by a checkpoint, taking you from a story you hope is true
to a case you can defend under attack.

## The sequence

### Step 1 - Pressure-test the raise decision itself (skill: apply-ai-native-models)
Before you assemble the proof, run the call through the OS's mental models. Is the thing you are
about to raise on actually AI-native, or a feature with good PR? Does the demo prove anything about
demand? The skill produces a one-page decision memo that scores the bet and names the riskiest
assumption, so you are not assembling evidence for a case that does not hold.

**Produces:** a one-page decision memo scoring the raise against the OS's mental models, with the
riskiest assumption named.

**CHECKPOINT 1.** Does the memo expose a hole the deck would have to hide? If the riskiest
assumption is one you cannot yet evidence, that is the gap to close before you pitch, not the line
to spin. An investor finds the hole faster than you can paper over it. Name it and decide whether the
rest of the flow can fill it.

### Step 2 - Prove the moat (skill: moat-strategy)
The first question in the room is what stops a giant cloning this next quarter. The skill produces a
moat thesis: which moat you are actually building, why it is durable, and the closed loop that feeds
it, written as one sentence of use to proprietary data to a sharper product. It ledgers the moat
against four tracking questions. This is the answer to the clone question, on paper, before it is
asked.

**Produces:** a moat thesis (which moat, why durable, the loop that feeds it) and a MOATS-LEDGER
entry against the four tracking questions.

**CHECKPOINT 2.** Run the Remove-the-AI test on the whole company. Delete the model call: is what
remains a flywheel of proprietary data and earned trust, or a generic chatbot? If it is a chatbot,
you do not have a moat slide yet, you have a wish, and no PMF number rescues it. Do not proceed on a
feature dressed as a moat.

### Step 3 - Show real PMF, not a launch spike (skill: measure-ai-native-pmf)
A moat with no demand is a defended empty room. The skill reads PMF across four lenses: the 40%
test, the Remove-the-AI test, Share of Model, and a second-bite retention curve. The fifth purchase
measures belief where the first measured curiosity. This is the traction slide built on returning
users, not on a total-users line that climbs whether or not anyone came back.

**Produces:** a PMF read grounded in real use: the 40% test, retention on the closed loop, and a
Share of Model baseline.

**CHECKPOINT 3.** Is the signal demand or a launch-week spike? Separate the users who returned from
the users who tried it once. A retention curve that flattens above zero is a story; one that decays
to nothing is a warning you put in the deck honestly rather than hide. If PMF is weak, the proof is
that the bet needs more work, and an investor respects that more than a number they can puncture.

### Step 4 - Survive the adversarial test (skill: red-team-the-agent)
An AI-native investor in a sensitive domain will ask whether your agent holds against someone trying
to break it. The skill builds the red-team attack suite, scores each attack by severity against
exploitability, and wires a pass/fail release gate. A system that has met a hostile user in the lab,
and held, is evidence. A system that has only met friendly demos is a liability waiting for its
first incident report.

**Produces:** a red-team attack suite, a severity-by-exploitability scoring rubric, and a pass/fail
result on the release gate.

**CHECKPOINT 4 - proof before hype.** Take every claim in the deck and put it through a web check.
The market size, the conversion rate, the regulatory timeline, the benchmark, the named comparison:
each survives the check or comes out of the deck. Never state a guess as fact and never invent a
figure, a company, or a quote to fill a slide; a fabricated number is the fastest way to lose the
room and your credibility with it. A claim that cannot survive the check is hype, and hype is what
this flow exists to remove. If a claim fails, fix the claim or cut it. Only a deck where every line
survives the check is ready to raise on.

## What the flow outputs

The evidence an AI-native investor expects, assembled and survivable:

- **The closed loop**, named and traced, from the moat thesis.
- **The moat**, ranked by durability and ledgered, with the Remove-the-AI verdict that the company
  collapses without its intelligence.
- **A real PMF signal**: the 40% test, retention on the loop, and a Share of Model baseline, built
  on returning users.
- **A system that survives adversarial testing**: a red-team suite run, scored, and gated.
- **A deck where every claim survives a web check**, with contested figures sourced.

This is preparation, not a recommendation to raise. The discipline is the same one that runs through
the whole OS: proof before hype, and a demo is not evidence. When the raise produces its first real
outcome, a term sheet, a hard question you could answer because the proof was already built, a claim
an investor checked and found true, run the `capture-learning` skill so the lesson lands in the
knowledge base dated and sourced.
