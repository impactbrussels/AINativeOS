# AI-Native OS for founders who do not code

You can use everything here without writing a line of code. No terminal, no install, no developer
tools. If you can copy a block of text and paste it into a chat box, you can run the whole method.

This guide shows you the three ways in, the two rules that make them work, and a first thirty minutes
that gets you a real result.

## You do not need a terminal

AI-Native OS is a method, written so a human can follow it and an AI can run it. The fancy version
lives inside coding tools. The plain version is a set of **prompts**: blocks of instruction you paste
into the same chatbots you already use. The thinking is identical. Only the wrapper changes.

So forget the install page unless you want it. Your starting point is the [Prompt
Library](../prompts/README.md) and the [start-here skill](../skills/start-here/SKILL.md).

## The three ways to use it without code

### 1. Copy a prompt into a chatbot

The simplest path, and the one to start with.

1. Open the [Prompt Library](../prompts/README.md). It lists the starter prompts by stage.
2. Pick one, open the file, and copy the prompt block.
3. Paste it into Claude.ai, ChatGPT, or Gemini.
4. Replace the `[PLACEHOLDERS]` with your real details (more on those below).
5. Send it, read the answer, and push back. Ask for the harder version. Ask it to argue against you.

Use these chat tools for thinking work: framing your idea, discovery, positioning, strategy, copy.
Anything that is words rather than a running app.

### 2. Use a build tool with the build-tagged prompts

When you want a working web page or a clickable app rather than a document, the no-code builders do
that. The prompts meant for them are tagged `build` in the library.

- **Lovable, Bolt, and v0** turn a clear, scoped brief into a working web app or landing page.
- **Replit Agent** goes a step further when you need something that actually runs in the background.

The move is the same: copy a `build`-tagged prompt, paste it into the builder, fill the
`[PLACEHOLDERS]`, and let it generate. Design your idea in a chat tool first, then ship it in a build
tool. Design in one place, build in the other.

### 3. Start with start-here to find your stage

If you do not know which prompt you need, do not guess. Open the
[start-here skill](../skills/start-here/SKILL.md), copy its copy-paste prompt, and paste it into any
chatbot. It asks you a few questions, runs the Remove-the-AI test on your idea, and points you to the
single next thing to do. It is the front door. When in doubt, knock here.

> **The Remove-the-AI test, in one line:** take the AI out of your product. If it still works, you
> built an AI feature, not an AI-native company. The whole method pushes you toward an idea that
> breaks without its intelligence, because that is where the moat is.

## How to fill the [PLACEHOLDERS]

Every prompt has bracketed words like `[PROBLEM]`, `[ICP]`, or `[DOMAIN]`. They are blanks for you to
fill with your real details. `[ICP]` means your ideal customer profile, the specific person you serve.
`[DOMAIN]` is your sector. `[PROBLEM]` is the pain you remove.

Replace each bracket with something concrete. Not "small businesses" but "independent physiotherapy
clinics in Belgium with two to five staff." The more specific you are, the sharper the answer. If a
term is new to you, the [Dictionary](../dictionary/) explains it in plain words.

## The two rules that make the prompts work

These are small and they matter more than anything else.

1. **Fill every placeholder.** A prompt with `[PROBLEM]` left in the text gives you generic mush. The
   AI cannot read your mind through a bracket. Specifics in, value out.
2. **Never trust an invented number.** If the AI states a market size, a regulatory timeline, or a
   competitor's revenue, treat it as a guess until proven. Ask it to label the source or call it an
   assumption, then check the real figure yourself. A confident wrong number is the expensive kind.

## Your first thirty minutes

A walkthrough that ends with something real in your hands.

1. **Minutes 0 to 5.** Open the [start-here skill](../skills/start-here/SKILL.md). Copy its
   copy-paste prompt into Claude.ai, ChatGPT, or Gemini. Answer its questions in one or two honest
   sentences each.
2. **Minutes 5 to 15.** It will run the Remove-the-AI test and name your stage. Follow its pointer to
   one skill. Most early founders land on
   [frame-the-hypothesis](../skills/frame-the-hypothesis/SKILL.md). Open that skill and copy its
   prompt.
3. **Minutes 15 to 25.** Fill the placeholders with your real idea and run it. You now have your
   vague idea written as one testable bet, with a built-in devil's advocate trying to refute it.
4. **Minutes 25 to 30.** Read the pushback honestly. Pick the single sharpest objection. That is your
   next piece of homework, and you found it without spending a euro or writing a line of code.

That is the loop: pick a stage, run a prompt, get a real artefact, follow the next pointer. Repeat it
as your idea grows.

## Where the Handbook and Dictionary help

- The **[Handbook](../handbook/)** is the method in depth. When a skill gives you the *what*, the
  matching handbook chapter gives you the *why*, with real companies that did it. Read the chapter for
  your stage when you want the full reasoning, not just the steps.
- The **[Dictionary](../dictionary/)** is your glossary. Any term that trips you up (context window,
  agent, data flywheel, Share of Model) is defined there in plain language, with what it means and
  what to avoid. Keep it open in a tab.

You do not need to read either cover to cover. Use them the way you use a map: when you are unsure
where you are, look, then keep moving.
