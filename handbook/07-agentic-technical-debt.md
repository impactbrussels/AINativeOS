# 07. Managing Agentic Technical Debt Before It Kills You

> **Thesis.** In a machine-learning system, changing anything changes everything. Pay down the debt that blocks scaling or blocks data collection first, or the thing you shipped fast becomes the thing that cannot move.

## The shift

Cheap code created a new kind of debt, and it does not behave like the old kind. In ordinary software, debt sits in one place and waits. You can draw a line around a module and say: change inside this line, nothing outside it moves. That line is the contract, and it holds because a human wrote both sides of it on purpose.

In a system built from models and agents, that line does not hold. The intelligence at the centre is a learned thing, and a learned thing entangles whatever it touches. Signals feed signals. One agent quietly comes to expect the shape of another's output. A cache freezes a field's old form. None of these couplings were designed. They grew, session by session, under deadline, while you were looking elsewhere. So a small, surgical, obviously correct fix in one corner degrades a model three hops away that you would swear is unrelated, and you are debugging at midnight, certain the system has a ghost in it.

It does not have a ghost. It has agentic technical debt, the specific form that compounds when the intelligence is load-bearing. This is the Build-stage counterweight to the two chapters before it. Chapter 5 told you to architect first, so the debt has somewhere to not accumulate. Chapter 6 told you to build with specs and tests. This chapter is about the debt that builds anyway, because in an AI-native system some of the entanglement is structural, and about how to keep it from compounding faster than you can pay it off. Ignore it and you do not get killed loudly. You get killed when every change is risky enough that the safest move becomes to change nothing. A product that cannot move is already dead.

## The framework

Five principles separate a system that keeps moving from one that seizes up.

1. **CACE, changing anything changes everything.** In a system with a learned component there is no such thing as a local change. Touch one input signal, one feature, one prompt, one model version, and you have potentially altered the behaviour of everything downstream, including the parts you would swear are unrelated. The acronym is blunt on purpose: assume entanglement until proven otherwise. This is not folklore. It is the central finding of Sculley and colleagues at Google, "Hidden Technical Debt in Machine Learning Systems" (NeurIPS 2015), the paper that named the trap most founders are now walking into. A model has no contract. It learned to lean on all its inputs, including the weird and slightly broken ones, so fixing a quirk can shift outputs everywhere at once. Stop sizing risk by how big the change looks. Ask instead: what is downstream of this, and is any of it learned? That question, before every change, is the whole discipline. The term: [CACE, changing anything changes everything](../dictionary/05-failure-modes.md#cace-changing-anything-changes-everything).

2. **Model drift and entanglement.** A model is a photograph of a moment. The world keeps moving, the photograph does not, and performance bleeds away a fraction at a time, invisibly, because no single day looks broken. That is [model drift](../dictionary/05-failure-modes.md#model-drift). Drift alone you could manage: retrain and move on. What makes it vicious is [entanglement](../dictionary/05-failure-modes.md#entanglement), the same undesigned coupling that powers CACE. Tangled signals smear the rot across everything downstream, so the symptom surfaces far from the cause. You see smoke in one room while the fire burns in another. The consequence is operational: watch inputs and intermediate signals, not just final outputs, or drift will have rotted three things before the output moves enough to alarm you.

3. **Context persistence.** Agentic debt is rarely one bad decision. It is a thousand small, reasonable trade-offs, each made under pressure, each forgotten the moment it was made. The fix is almost embarrassingly low-tech. Keep a master file, your `CLAUDE.md`, the same one the agents already read, and update it daily with the trade-offs you made and the logic-debt you deliberately deferred. Not the code. The decisions about the code. "Hard-coded the supplier list, proper config is debt, ticket 41." "Skipped the retry on the parse step, fine until 10x volume." This converts invisible debt into visible debt, and visible debt can be ranked, scheduled, and paid. It also keeps your agents safe: an agent that reads the file inherits every trade-off, so it stops reinventing your codebase from a blank page and stops "fixing" the shortcut that is holding the system up. The category is [agentic technical debt](../dictionary/05-failure-modes.md#agentic-technical-debt); the daily file is the project's [memory system](../dictionary/02-context-and-sessions.md#memory-system) pointed at its own construction.

4. **ROI-driven paydown.** You will have debt. The question that separates the founders who survive growth from the ones who get buried is not whether you have debt. It is whether you know which debt to pay first. Treat debt the way debt actually works, as a thing with an interest rate, and pay the highest-interest debt first however quiet it is. Ugly code that works and never changes is almost free, so leave it. An architectural flaw that blocks scaling is expensive. A flaw that blocks data collection is the most expensive debt you own, because in an AI-native company the data flywheel is the moat, and every day a dropped signal or a silently open loop persists, you throw away learning a competitor can never recover. Rank by two questions that sit above all the aesthetic ones: which debt blocks scaling, and which blocks data collection. Pay those first, even when the ugly code is screaming. Especially then.

5. **The God Agent fallacy and guardrails.** One capable agent doing everything inside one giant prompt feels right early and becomes the most brittle, unauditable thing you own. A [God Agent](../dictionary/04-agentic-patterns.md#the-god-agent-anti-pattern) is maximum entanglement by construction: every instruction shares one context, so a rule added for the refund case changes the prompt the agent uses for everything, and you cannot test any capability in isolation because there is none. The fix is structural. Chain narrow specialists behind a [gateway agent](../dictionary/04-agentic-patterns.md#gateway-agent), a receptionist that reads the task, picks the right specialist, and holds no domain logic of its own. Then cap the blast radius of any single agent with two numbers set before you ship: a budget cap and an iteration limit. An agent meeting an unsolvable problem does not stop and ask for help. It re-plans, re-calls, loops, and at 3 a.m. with nobody watching it can run that loop ten thousand times before anyone sees the bill. That is the [infinite loop, runaway agent](../dictionary/05-failure-modes.md#infinite-loop-runaway-agent), and it is the default behaviour of an unbounded agent, not a rare edge case.

## Why it holds

Two companies show the framework working, and the contrast between them is the whole chapter. Treat the specific figures as illustrative of the pattern; they carry a `[claimed in source]` tag and are logged, with their origins, in [SOURCES.md](SOURCES.md). Weigh the shape, not the digits.

**Finery Markets**, in financial infrastructure, faced exactly the job that tempts a founder toward a God Agent: financial reporting, many steps deep. Gather the data, reconcile it, validate it, compose the report. The naive build is one big agent that does all of it. They went the other way and chained specialised agents, each owning one stage behind a routing layer. The result they report is a reporting process cut from roughly eight hours to about thirty minutes [claimed in source]. The speed is the headline. The maintainability is the point. A single God Agent doing eight-hour reporting would have been a nightmare to change, every edit to one report type risking every other, entanglement compounding with each new requirement. By splitting the work into specialists, they built something they can still safely modify next year. In a regulated, high-stakes domain, the agent fleet you can change beats the one that demos faster.

**Zapier** built its business on static automation: when this happens, do that, a fixed if-then wired between apps. Reliable, and brittle in one specific way. The day a connected app's interface changes, or a step returns something unexpected, the automation breaks and sits there broken until a human notices. Its move into agents answers that brittleness directly: agents that handle errors and re-plan when the ground shifts, instead of failing silently and waiting to be rescued. This is drift made operational. Static automation is the purest victim of the world refusing to hold still; an agent that can observe a failure and re-plan is the adaptive response. But carry the other half. That same adaptability is a source of agentic debt when it is unbounded. An agent that re-plans freely is an agent that can loop, which is precisely why the guardrails in principle five are not optional. The more your system can adapt on its own, the more it can run away on its own. Build the adaptability, then build the cage around it.

## In hard mode

Two things change weight in food, health, and deeptech, and neither one retrofits.

Drift in a clinical or food-safety model is not a bug. It is a safety event. Your moat is usually a model tuned to your own corpus: assay results, regulatory documents, a patient cohort, a supply chain. That corpus is exactly the kind of thing that drifts, because protocols update, regulations get rewritten, and the population you serve changes. The mechanism that makes the product defensible is the same one that makes it decay, and when it decays on a dose, an allergen, or a contamination threshold, the cost is not a degraded metric. It is a recall or a hospital visit. So drift detection is not a second-version nicety here. It is a held-out set you re-score on a fixed cadence, an alarm on input distributions, a human spot-check, and a flat performance line read as "monitored and confirmed fine" rather than just "fine."

The second thing is sharper. Debt that blocks data collection blocks the moat directly. In hard mode the proprietary, validated dataset is the entire defensible asset, the one thing a competitor renting the same model cannot reproduce without re-running your years of experiments. A signal that is not logged, a correction that goes nowhere, a closed loop that is silently open: each of those is not a code-quality problem. It is a hole in the one thing the giants cannot reach. Rank it accordingly. The highest-interest debt in a hard-mode company is almost always the quiet leak nobody is complaining about, because it costs nothing today and the moat tomorrow.

## What it means for you

1. **Run a debt audit monthly, and force the ranking.** Point your agentic-coding tool at the repo and have it find four things: hard-coded agent logic, hidden dependencies that feed more than one learned component, broken feedback loops where a signal is generated but never stored, and agents that can call a model or tool without a cap. Then make it sort everything by interest rate, highest being what blocks scaling or data collection. An unranked list of debt paralyses. A ranked one tells you where to spend your next free week.

2. **Make the daily master-file update a literal closing step.** At the end of every session, yours or an agent's, append to `CLAUDE.md` under a dated heading: the architectural trade-off you chose and what you gave up, the debt you deliberately deferred and the condition under which it needs fixing, and any new entanglement. A few honest lines. Two minutes. It is the difference between inheriting a maze and inheriting a map with notes in the margins.

3. **Break your God Agent apart, one specialist at a time.** Do not rewrite in one swing. Extract the most isolated job first, give it a short prompt and only the tools it needs, stand up a dumb gateway that classifies and hands off, then move the rest over one at a time. Watch the whole system's output as you go, not just the piece you moved, because CACE means the extraction itself can shift things.

4. **Keep the gateway dumb.** The moment the router starts carrying a little domain logic "just for this one case," you have rebuilt the God Agent with extra steps. The router routes. The specialists work. Hold that line.

5. **Set the caps low and raise them later.** A budget cap and an iteration limit on every agent, set before you ship, set tight. You can loosen a limit that proves too strict. You cannot un-spend what a runaway already burned.

## The test

Name your system's top three debts, then write the interest rate of each in one line: does it block scaling, does it block data collection, or is it merely cosmetic. If your number-one debt turns out to be a broken feedback loop or a missing signal that nobody was complaining about, that is not a coincidence. That is the chapter working. The debt that screams is rarely the debt that kills you. The [`pay-down-agentic-debt`](../skills/pay-down-agentic-debt/SKILL.md) skill is the runnable version of this drill: run it to rate each debt by its interest and schedule the paydown. Pair it with [`architect-before-code`](../skills/architect-before-code/SKILL.md) to re-establish the System Inventory, because you cannot rank what is downstream of a change until you hold the map of what is downstream of what.
