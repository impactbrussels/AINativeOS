# 12. Measuring Real Product-Market Fit, Not AI Hype

> **Thesis.** Vanity metrics are dead. Real fit is the 40% test, plus the Remove-the-AI test, plus rising Share of Model. The first purchase measures curiosity; the fifth measures belief. A hype number that cannot survive a web check is a warning, not a win.

## The shift

Total users went up and to the right, and it told you nothing. That was always the lie of the registration chart: a number that grows whether or not a single person came back, whether or not anyone would miss the product if it vanished overnight. AI has made the lie cheaper to tell. A model writes a slick onboarding flow, a launch post lands on the right feed, and ten thousand people try the thing in a week. The graph looks like fit. It is curiosity with good distribution, and the two are easy to confuse right up until the renewal date.

The confusion is worse now because the hype itself has a market. A founder can post a number, a model can dress it as a thesis, and an engine can repeat it to the next buyer before anyone thinks to check. "Revenue per employee," "users in week one," "AI-powered everything": the genre writes itself, and it writes fast. Some of it is real. Much of it is a headline engineered to be quoted, with the quoting now free and the checking still expensive. That asymmetry is the whole problem. The cost of producing an impressive number fell to nothing while the cost of verifying one stayed exactly where it was, so the impressive numbers multiply and the honest ones drown.

So the question the metric has to answer changed. Not "how many tried it?" but "would they grieve if it went away?" Not "did the AI demo well?" but "does the product still work when the model is gone, and is the part that breaks yours?" Not "how much traffic?" but "how often does the engine cite you when the buyer asks?" Behaviour, money, and citation share are signals. Total users and total traffic are decoration. The rest of this chapter is the difference between the two.

## The framework

Five tests separate real product-market fit from a number engineered to be quoted.

1. **The 40% rule (Sean Ellis test).** Ask your active users one question: how would you feel if you could no longer use the product? You have product-market fit when more than 40% answer "very disappointed." The threshold is attributed to Sean Ellis, and it works because it measures dependence rather than enthusiasm. A user can rate you five stars and never return. The one who would be very disappointed has already built you into a routine, and a routine is the only thing churn cannot easily take back. Survey the people who actually use the product, not the ones who signed up and drifted, or you will measure the wrong room.

2. **The Remove-the-AI test as a product-market-fit lens.** Take the model out of your product. What still works, and is the part that breaks yours? The same test that exposes a wrapper in the architecture exposes a hollow metric in the funnel. If the AI is removed and the product still does its job, you measured demand for a feature anyone can rent, not fit for something only you have. Strong fit and a hollow core can coexist for a while, which is exactly the trap: people love the convenience, the convenience is one API call, and the renewal belongs to whoever undercuts you next quarter. Run the test on what they are paying for, not on what you built.

3. **Share of Model.** This is the new top of the funnel. Share of Model is how often an AI engine names you, versus a rival, when a buyer asks a real question in your category. Run your customers' actual queries through the engines they use; count whether each answer cites you, cites a competitor, or cites neither. That ratio is your visibility in the channel where considered buying increasingly starts, and unlike an ad impression it is earned by being the source a model trusts to be right. A rising share means the category's answer is becoming your answer. A falling one means a competitor is being quoted in rooms you cannot see.

4. **The second bite.** The first purchase measures curiosity. The fifth measures belief. Most companies design for trial and starve repeat: a launch budget, a discount, a demo that converts a stranger once, and then nothing built to earn the return. That is a funnel optimised for the wrong bite. A curious first purchase tells you a person was willing to find out. Only the repeat tells you the product entered their life. The gap between those two facts is where most founders fool themselves, because the first bite is loud and the second is quiet, and the loud one is the one the dashboard celebrates. Real product-market fit includes a retention curve that proves trial became habit: the cohort from January still here in May, the line flattening into a floor instead of sliding to zero, the reorder happening without a coupon to prompt it. A product people need pulls the second bite out of them. A product people merely tried has to keep buying it back with discounts until the discounts run out. Build the second bite into the metric, or you will celebrate a number that is already leaving.

5. **Vanity versus real signal.** Three things are real: behaviour, money, and citation share. People returning unprompted, people paying again, engines naming you. Two things are decoration: total users and total traffic, both of which grow whether or not anyone stayed. The discipline is to throw out the decoration and keep the three. And one rule sits above all of it. A hype number that fails a web check is a warning, not a win. If a figure cannot survive ten minutes of independent verification, it does not belong in your deck, your homepage, or your own private read of how the company is doing. The number you cannot prove is the number that will be used against you.

## Why it holds

Two companies show the gap between a citation you earned and a number you posted.

**OpenEvidence** earned its position the slow way, by being right where being wrong is unforgivable. It answers clinical questions for physicians, and the reason doctors act on the answers is that every one is tied to the medical literature: the system surfaces a precise, evidence-based reply with the source attached, rather than improvising a confident paragraph. That is the difference between a tool a clinician trusts and a chatbot a clinician ignores. Run the Remove-the-AI test and the model is rentable, but the grounding, the corpus, and the citation discipline are the product. The market reflects it. The tool is reported as used by a large share of US physicians, with a valuation reported at roughly 12 billion dollars in early 2026 [claimed in source]. Read those figures as the order of magnitude of trust earned, not a benchmark to plan against. The transferable point needs no number: precise, sourced answers in a domain that punishes error are how you turn the 40% question from a hope into a habit.

**Medvi** is the cautionary half of the pair, and it earns its place here by being exactly the kind of number this chapter tells you to distrust. The widely circulated claim is roughly 401 million dollars in revenue from a telehealth operation with about two employees [claimed in source, unverified]. It is the perfect hype metric: astonishing, quotable, and shaped to confirm the thesis that AI has decoupled output from headcount. Then the verification arrives. As reported, the framing is heavily contested; an FDA warning letter over misbranding (February 2026) predates the profile that made the number famous, and multiple outlets allege the story was overstated, with some calling it scam-adjacent (as reported). The lesson is not that the figure is definitely false. The lesson is that you could not have known from the headline, and the headline was engineered so you would not check. A number that astonishing demands a web check before it earns a single share. Treat any metric that flatters the moment the way OpenEvidence treats a clinical claim: nothing ships until the source resolves.

| Signal | Real or decoration | What it actually measures |
|---|---|---|
| Total registered users | Decoration | How good your launch post was |
| Website traffic | Decoration | How wide your distribution reached |
| 40% "very disappointed" | Real | Dependence: would they grieve the loss |
| Repeat purchase / retention curve | Real | Whether trial became habit |
| Revenue per returning cohort | Real | Money following belief, not curiosity |
| Share of Model | Real | Citation in the channel where buying starts |
| A figure that fails a web check | Warning | That someone hoped you would not check |

## In hard mode

In consumer food and health, trial is not habit, and the gap between them is where the category is actually won or lost.

A curious first purchase tells you almost nothing. Someone saw the packaging, read the claim, wanted to find out, and bought once. That is the launch working, not the product working. The whole economics of a food or supplement brand sit one bite further on: the second purchase, the reorder, the line on the retention curve that refuses to fall to zero. A novel protein, a fermented ingredient, a longevity supplement can all post a triumphant launch week and be dead by the third reorder, because curiosity is a one-time event and habit is a hundred small repeats. The 40% question lands hardest here. Would the buyer be very disappointed to lose it, or did they try it once, feel clever, and move on?

This is also where hype metrics do the most damage, because the regulated buyer in front of you has seen the genre before. Cite a launch number to a food-safety director or a clinical partner and you have told them you measure the wrong thing. The number that moves them is the retention curve and the citation, both of which survive a web check, neither of which a launch budget can manufacture. The second bite is the real product-market fit in these sectors precisely because a first purchase tells you whether the marketing landed, and only the repeat tells you whether the product entered a life.

## What it means for you

1. **Run the 40% survey on active users, this month.** Ask the [40% question](../dictionary/07-ai-native-business.md#the-40-rule-sean-ellis-test) of people who genuinely use the product, not the full sign-up list. Under 40% "very disappointed" is not a verdict to hide from. It is a map of which segment already depends on you, and that segment is where the next version should aim.

2. **Run the [Remove-the-AI test](../dictionary/07-ai-native-business.md#the-remove-the-ai-test) on your fit, not just your stack.** Ask what your repeat buyers are actually paying to keep. If the answer is convenience the model provides and a competitor can rent, your retention is borrowed. Push the product toward the part that breaks without your data, your corpus, your [data flywheel](../dictionary/07-ai-native-business.md#data-flywheel), because that is the part the renewal is loyal to. Wrapper or [native](../dictionary/07-ai-native-business.md#ai-native-vs-wrapper) is a question your retention curve answers honestly.

3. **Track [Share of Model](../dictionary/07-ai-native-business.md#share-of-model) monthly, as a real number.** Pick the queries your buyers ask, run them through the engines, and count the citations. Watch the trend, not the snapshot. A share that climbs while your traffic is flat is worth more than traffic that climbs while your share erodes, because the first is the channel that decides and the second is the channel that used to.

4. **Build the second bite into the dashboard.** Put the retention curve next to the acquisition number and read them together. A spike in trial with a flat repeat is a warning dressed as a win. Design one concrete thing that earns the fifth purchase, ship it, and measure whether the cohort line bends upward. Trial is the cost of finding out. Habit is the business.

5. **Web-check every number before it leaves the building.** Any figure you put in a deck, a homepage, or a board update has to survive an independent search first. If a model drafted the claim, resolve the source yourself; a confident citation that points at nothing is a [hallucination](../dictionary/05-failure-modes.md#hallucination) you signed your name to. The [verification deficit](../dictionary/05-failure-modes.md#verification-deficit) is how a flattering number becomes a shipped lie. Close it before you publish, not after a journalist does it for you.

## The test

Look at the metric you are proudest of. Would it survive ten minutes of independent verification by someone who wanted to prove you wrong, and does it measure the fifth purchase or only the first? If it is a launch number that flatters the moment and cannot survive a web check, you are measuring curiosity and calling it fit. Find the repeat, prove the dependence, earn the citation. → Run `measure-ai-native-pmf`.
