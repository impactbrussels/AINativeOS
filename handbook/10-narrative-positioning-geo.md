# 10. Narrative, Positioning & Founder Brand (GEO)

> **Thesis.** Be cited, not ranked. The new top of funnel is Share of Model, how often the AI engines name you for the buyer's question. And never let the narrative outrun the proof, because the gap between the story you tell and the evidence you can show is exactly where trust dies.

## The shift

For twenty years, getting found meant ranking on a page of blue links a human then chose to click. That page is being replaced. Your buyer opens Perplexity, or ChatGPT, or Claude, types a question in plain English, and reads four confident sentences that name three companies as proof. The engine is not handing them a list to scroll. It is writing the answer and deciding who gets named inside it.

This breaks the old playbook quietly, which is why most founders miss it. You can still rank. Your page still exists, still carries the keywords, still sits somewhere on a results page nobody runs anymore. None of that puts your name in the paragraph the buyer actually read. The engine did not rank you and skip you. It synthesised an answer from someone else's words, and you were never in the room where the synthesis happened.

The surface moved and the winners changed with it. An engine that quotes rather than ranks rewards different content: a claim that stands on its own, a statistic with a source attached, depth a keyword-stuffer cannot fake. So the work is no longer to climb a list. It is to become the source the machine reaches for. That is the first half of this chapter.

The second half is a warning, and it is Adam's framework. Winning the citation is not the same as deserving it. A bold narrative gets you named early, in the answer and in the room. But the moment your buyer acts on the story and the product underdelivers, the distance between what you said and what you can prove becomes the thing they remember. Get cited for a claim you cannot stand behind and you have not won the channel. You have armed the engine to repeat your overclaim to everyone who asks next.

## The framework

Five moves win the new channel without outrunning your proof.

1. **GEO (Generative Engine Optimisation).** Optimise to be cited inside the answer an AI engine writes, not merely listed in a page of links a human might scroll. A search engine ranked pages and let the human choose. A generative engine reads across the web, writes one answer in its own voice, and names a handful of sources as evidence. The unit of victory changes from rank to citation, and citation rewards content a model can lift cleanly and attribute with confidence. See [GEO (Generative Engine Optimisation)](../dictionary/07-ai-native-business.md#geo-generative-engine-optimization).

2. **Extractability.** Structure each section so it opens with a standalone, complete answer a crawler can quote without the surrounding context. A model does not savour your introduction. It scans for the chunk that answers the question and grabs the chunk that stands alone. Bury your best claim in paragraph six, after the warm-up, and the engine never lifts it, because by then the sentence leans on five sentences above it that the model did not pull. Lead every section with its answer. A self-contained sentence is liftable. One that needs its neighbours is not. See [extractability](../dictionary/07-ai-native-business.md#extractability).

3. **Citation factors.** Three qualities make an engine pick your words over a rival's: named-source statistics, authoritative expert quotes, and genuine technical depth. They share one thing. They are signals of trust a machine can detect. An engine building a careful answer is managing its own risk, so it gravitates to sources that make the answer defensible and away from content written to be found. A statistic with its source named in the same breath is liftable as a fact; the same number floating free reads as the kind of thing that gets a model caught [hallucinating](../dictionary/05-failure-modes.md#hallucination), and it would rather not risk you.

4. **Share of Model.** Measure how often the AI engines cite you versus competitors for your buyer queries. Take the actual questions your buyers ask, run them through ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Claude, and for each answer count whether it names you, names a rival, or names neither. That ratio is your visibility in the channel where considered buying now begins. It is the new top-of-funnel number, and it moves when the GEO work lands. See [Share of Model](../dictionary/07-ai-native-business.md#share-of-model).

5. **The narrative-evidence gap.** This is the distance between the story you tell and the proof you can show, and it is the discipline the other four make dangerous to ignore. A small gap is healthy. It pulls attention forward, gives early buyers a reason to lean in, lets you raise and hire on a vision the product has not fully reached yet. A wide, unclosed gap is a trap. The day the buyer acts on the story and the product underdelivers, the gap is what they feel, and trust does not erode politely. It collapses. GEO makes this worse, not better, because the engine you taught to quote your boldest claim will repeat that claim to every buyer after the one you disappointed. Close the gap deliberately: pace the narrative to the proof you can produce, and every time you widen the story, ship the evidence that catches up to it.

## Why it holds

Two patterns, four companies. Two show the citation game won. Two show the gap left open.

**Intercom**, with Paul Adams as chief product officer, framed the arrival of AI-native software not as a feature update but as a platform change, the kind that resets the ground a whole category is built on. They moved the bulk of research and development to AI and described customer service as something about to be remade rather than improved [claimed in source]. The move worth studying is the scale of the claim. By naming the shift at the size of an era, Intercom positioned itself as the company defining the new category rather than reacting to it, and the company that names the category gets quoted when buyers, analysts, and engines reach for language to describe it. Narrative at category scale is not hype when you are genuinely early. It is how you become the reference everyone else ends up citing.

**CodeRabbit** and **7AI** built visibility in technical markets substantially by appearing in the answers engineers got when they asked AI engines about a class of problem, rather than by banking the years of traditional domain authority older rivals had [claimed in source]. CodeRabbit, an AI code-review tool founded in early 2023, is a verifiable case of a newcomer reaching buyers through the new surface rather than the old one. That is the GEO thesis demonstrated: the new channel has different winners, and a newcomer with genuinely deep, extractable technical content can be cited next to incumbents who outrank them everywhere on Google. You do not have to win the old authority game to win the new citation game.

Now the other pattern, where the narrative ran ahead of the evidence.

**Oatly** built a category-defining climate narrative, and in 2022 the UK Advertising Standards Authority upheld complaints against several of its adverts, ruling that claims including a "73% less CO2e" comparison against milk breached the advertising code on misleading and environmental claims. The point is not that Oatly was cynical. The point is structural. The narrative made a specific, quotable, checkable claim, and the proof behind that exact claim did not hold to the standard a regulator applied. That is the narrative-evidence gap made literal: a story sharp enough to be cited, and a substantiation that could not carry it.

**Beyond Meat** rode a category narrative, plant-based meat as an inevitable mainstream replacement, that ran ahead of repeat-purchase reality. The story sold the first bite. It did not, at the scale the narrative implied, secure the fifth. As demand softened, net revenue fell across 2022 and 2023: down about ten percent in 2022, and lower again the year after. The lesson is not that the vision was wrong. It is that a narrative measured by trial, when the product is measured by habit, opens a gap that the market eventually prices in. The story can win the citation and still lose the customer.

## In hard mode

In food, health, and deeptech, the narrative-evidence gap is not enforced by disappointed customers alone. It is enforced by regulators, and they have teeth.

An overclaim in a meme app is a bad review. The same overclaim on a food label, a supplement, or a health benefit is an Advertising Standards Authority ruling, an EFSA problem, or a regulatory action that pulls the claim, the advert, or the product. Oatly did not lose a customer's goodwill over its carbon comparison. It lost a ruling. In a regulated sector the gap between story and proof is adjudicated by a body that can compel you to stop saying the thing, and the adjudication is public, permanent, and quotable, which means the engines can cite the ruling too.

This raises the stakes on GEO precisely where GEO matters most. Your hardest-won knowledge, the regulatory nuance, the assay detail, the thing that took a decade to learn, is your strongest citation asset, because it is the depth a thin competitor cannot fake. But the same depth invites scrutiny. The moment you publish a claim extractable enough for an engine to quote, you have published it in a form a regulator can quote back at you. So the discipline is not to soften the claim. It is to close the gap under it. Write the bold, specific, citable sentence, and make sure the dossier behind it would survive the body that polices your sector. In hard mode, the extractable claim and the load-bearing proof have to be the same length.

## What it means for you

1. **Write the one-sentence extractable answer to your category's top buyer query.** The exact question a high-intent buyer types into an AI engine at the start of their research, and the one or two sentences you want quoted back, standing completely on their own, naming you or your category-defining claim. That sentence is your GEO north star. Every pillar page opens with a version of it.
2. **Lead every section with its answer, then pour the depth underneath.** You are writing two documents at once: a quotable headline for the machine, and the depth for the person the machine sends you. Audit your pages for sections that cannot surrender a standalone sentence, and fix those first, because those are the sections the engines read past.
3. **Attach a named source to every number and a name to every expert claim.** Unsourced statistics read as risk and get skipped. The depth, the caveat, the failure mode is the signal a thin competitor cannot fake, so write it down extractably and let it become your distribution advantage.
4. **Baseline your Share of Model before you run a campaign.** Pick the twenty queries your buyers actually type, score them across the engines, write the number down, and re-score on a cadence. A single reading lies; a monthly series tells the truth. Treat a flat line as confirmed flat, not as fine.
5. **Measure the narrative-evidence gap and close it on purpose.** For every bold claim in your story, name the proof that backs it and the proof you still owe. Pace the narrative to what you can produce. When you widen the story, ship the evidence that catches up to it, before a buyer, or a regulator, finds the gap for you.

## The test

Take your single boldest marketing claim, the one you most want an AI engine to quote, and ask: if my most sceptical buyer acted on this sentence today, would the product, the data, and the dossier behind it hold, or would they find the gap? If you cannot answer with proof you could put in front of a regulator, your narrative is outrunning your evidence, and the engine you taught to quote it will spread the gap faster than you can close it. Run the [`geo-content`](../skills/geo-content/SKILL.md) skill (Stage: Grow) to make each claim extractable and sourced, then run [`measure-ai-native-pmf`](../skills/measure-ai-native-pmf/SKILL.md) for the Share-of-Model baseline, because you cannot tell whether your narrative is landing until you have counted how often the engines name you versus your competitors.
