# Case Study: NotCo

*The food-tech company where every taste-test makes the next product easier: the data flywheel, edible.*

**Sector:** food · **AI-native pattern:** data flywheel + R&D de-risking

## What they do

NotCo makes plant-based versions of animal foods, milk, mayo, burgers, and more, but its real engine sits a layer beneath the products. At the centre is a proprietary generative-AI system the company commonly refers to as "Giuseppe." Giuseppe's job is to answer a brutally hard question: given the taste, texture, and behaviour of an animal-based food, which combination of plants will reproduce it? It does this by analysing molecular and sensory data and matching the target across a vast space of plant ingredients that no human R&D team could search by hand.

The reason this matters is that conventional food R&D is slow, expensive, and mostly guesswork. A food scientist forms a hunch, mixes a batch, tastes it, adjusts, and repeats, for months. Most batches fail. The cost of a plant-based product line is the cost of all those dead ends. NotCo's pitch is that Giuseppe shrinks the guessing: instead of a scientist proposing one formulation at a time from intuition, the model proposes candidates from data, and the lab tests the ones most likely to work. The search space is the same; the path through it is far shorter.

So NotCo is, on paper, a consumer packaged-goods company: it sells food on shelves. But its advantage isn't a recipe or a brand. It's the system that finds the recipes, and the accumulated record of every formulation it has ever tried.

## The AI-native lesson

Apply the [Remove-the-AI test](../../dictionary/07-ai-native-business.md#the-remove-the-ai-test): take Giuseppe out and NotCo becomes an ordinary CPG company: a perfectly normal plant-based brand doing R&D the slow, intuition-driven way, indistinguishable from a hundred others. Nothing about the milk carton is AI-native. The AI-native part is *how the carton came to exist*: a model matching flavour and texture across disparate molecular and sensory data, faster and wider than people can. That's the difference between [AI-native and a wrapper](../../dictionary/07-ai-native-business.md#ai-native-vs-wrapper). Pull the model and the company doesn't slow down, it reverts to being a different kind of business entirely.

NotCo is the textbook food-tech [data flywheel](../../dictionary/07-ai-native-business.md#data-flywheel). Every formulation Giuseppe proposes and the lab tests produces a result: it tasted right, or it didn't, and here's the molecular and sensory profile of why. That result feeds back into the model. The next prediction is sharper because the last experiment, success or failure, taught it something. So the system doesn't just use data; it *manufactures* its own training data with every product cycle. A new entrant could buy the same lab equipment and hire the same calibre of food scientists, but it could not buy the years of test results that make NotCo's model good. The moat isn't the algorithm. Algorithms diffuse. The moat is the proprietary history of what worked and what didn't, which only accrues to whoever ran the experiments.

This is why the pattern de-risks R&D in a way that compounds. Traditional food development's cost is the failed batches; NotCo turns failed batches into fuel. A failure that would be pure loss for a conventional CPG firm is, for NotCo, a labelled data point that improves the next prediction. Over enough cycles, the company that treats every experiment as training data pulls ahead of the one that treats experiments as one-off costs, and the gap widens, because the leader's flywheel spins faster. NotCo has reported using Giuseppe to develop products across multiple categories and partnerships, and has been valued in the billions at peak `[claimed in source]`; read those figures as signals of the pattern's pull, not as numbers to plan against: see [SOURCES.md](../SOURCES.md).

## What a founder should copy

- **Find the expensive, slow, guess-driven loop in your sector and make it a search.** NotCo's was formulation R&D. Yours might be something else entirely, but the move is the same: replace one-at-a-time intuition with model-proposed candidates.
- **Treat every experiment as training data, including the failures.** The failed batch that's a sunk cost for your competitor is a labelled example for you. Capture the *why*, not just the pass/fail.
- **Build the flywheel into the operation, not the pitch deck.** The data has to feed back automatically: predict, test, record, retrain. Otherwise you have a demo, not a compounding advantage.
- **Understand that your moat is the history, not the model.** Algorithms diffuse; your proprietary record of what worked does not. Protect and grow that record above all.
- **De-risk first, brand second.** NotCo's edge upstream (finding formulations cheaply) is what funds everything downstream. Win the R&D loop and the product line becomes a consequence, not a gamble.
